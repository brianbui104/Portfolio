Carbon-Fibre Front Bumper

Design and fabrication of a custom carbon-fibre front bumper for a Toyota MR2 Spyder, from mold prep to final fitment.

Personal project Composite automotive component Carbon-fibre • Vacuum bagging

Overview & motivation

I wanted to learn composites in a way that actually mattered to me, so I chose a high-commitment project: building a carbon-fibre front bumper for my Toyota MR2 Spyder. The goal wasn’t just aesthetics—it was to understand how mold prep, layup strategy, and vacuum bagging all come together in a real part with tight fit-up constraints.

The project started from the OEM bumper geometry and evolved into a custom design. I built up the tooling, laid up multiple plies of carbon, vacuum bagged and cured the part, then trimmed and finished it until it bolted onto the car like a production piece.

My responsibilities

  • Designed the outer shape and transitions of the new bumper.
  • Prepared a foam/fiberglass mold with tooling gel coat.
  • Planned fibre orientation and ply schedule for stiffness.
  • Handled vacuum bagging, curing, trimming, and finishing.
Mold building Composite layup Vacuum systems Fitment & finishing

Process & design decisions

I started by shaping the base form with foam and body filler, sculpting the bumper until it matched both the MR2’s lines and practical ground clearance. This plug was sealed and coated with tooling gel coat to form a durable mold surface, then backed with fiberglass for stiffness.

For the carbon layup, I used multiple plies with different orientations (0°/90° and ±45°) to balance in-plane stiffness with impact resistance. Careful attention went into corners and openings where bridging, resin pooling, and fibre distortion are common failure points.

The vacuum bag setup included release film, breather, and a full perimeter seal. I chased leaks, checked vacuum levels over time, and learned just how sensitive the process is to bag wrinkles and sharp radii in the mold.

  • Used foam sculpting to quickly iterate to a visually satisfying shape.
  • Tooling gel coat provided a smooth, polishable mold surface.
  • Balanced aesthetics with approach angle and daily driving constraints.
  • Planned trimming lines so final edges would land on flat flange regions.

Outcomes & lessons learned

The finished bumper fit the MR2 with only minor trimming and drilling. It was significantly lighter than the OEM part and stiff enough not to flap or deform at speed. The surface finish wasn’t perfect, but it was good enough to clear coat and proudly run on the car.

Most importantly, I came away with a realistic sense of how messy and sensitive composite work can be. I learned how much prep goes into a single layup, how a tiny leak can ruin a vacuum bag, and how design choices determine whether you’re fighting the material or working with it.
