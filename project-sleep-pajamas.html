Sleep Well, Age Well – Sleep Tracking Pajamas

Wearable sleep tracking system for retired adults in the GTA, designed to be comfortable, low-friction, and easy to adopt.

Problem & context

Our client wanted a way for retired adults in the GTA to monitor and understand their sleep without adding more screens or complicated devices. Existing solutions like watches, phones, and rings are often expensive, uncomfortable, or too tech-heavy for long-term adoption.

Our team’s final concept was a set of Sleep Tracking Pajamas: a familiar, low-friction garment with embedded sensors that collect sleep data passively while the user just wears what they already wear to bed.

Concept & design details

We embedded non-obtrusive sensors in a two-piece pajama set to track movement, basic vitals, and sleep state proxies. The design emphasized:

  • Comfort: soft, breathable fabrics with flat seams around sensors.
  • Washability: removable electronics module; garment remains machine-washable.
  • Safety & privacy: clear data handling, no live audio/video, encryption when stored or transmitted.
  • Ease of use: automatic logging; user only needs to plug in the module to charge.

The project followed the ESP framework: stakeholders, service environment, Functions–Objectives–Constraints (FOCs), and traceable decision-making from client needs to final concept.

Outcomes & learning

The Sleep Tracking Pajamas concept met the core needs identified in the client interviews: comfort, low friction, and meaningful feedback on sleep quality. Our final documents clearly traced how each design choice tied back to stakeholder needs and constraints.

Personally, this project taught me how valuable structured design thinking is: instead of jumping straight to a gadget, we spent time clarifying the problem, environment, and constraints so that our solution actually fit into users’ lives.
