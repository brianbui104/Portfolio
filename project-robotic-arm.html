Robotic Arm – MIE243 Final Project

Design of a 2-DOF differential joint and a 1-DOF worm-gear rotation joint for a cinematography-style robotic arm.

Course project (MIE243) Robotic joints & power transmission SolidWorks • Gear design

Overview & role

For the final MIE243 design project, our team developed a robotic arm aimed at executing repeatable camera motions that are difficult to achieve by hand. My focus was on the mechanical joint design, particularly a 2-DOF differential joint at the base and a 1-DOF worm-gear rotation joint further up the arm.

The goal was to create a compact, stiff, and realistically manufacturable joint architecture that could be driven by BLDC motors and transmit torque through gear trains and belts, while respecting workspace and clearance constraints.

My contributions

  • Concept generation and down-selection for joint architectures.
  • Modelling the differential and worm-gear joints in SolidWorks.
  • Defining load cases and basic sizing for gears and bearings.
  • Documenting design decisions and rationale in the final report.
SolidWorks Differential mechanisms Worm gears Bearings & belts

Design highlights

The base joint uses a 2-DOF differential to combine the output of two motors into coordinated motion. The differential is rigidly connected to a planetary gearbox so the base of the arm can achieve 3-DOF motion while keeping most of the mass close to the base, improving stability and inertia.

Higher up the arm, a worm-gear rotation joint drives belts to transmit motion. The worm gear is intentionally hard to backdrive, so when the arm is holding a pose under load it behaves like it has a built-in brake. This reduces control effort and avoids needing a separate locking mechanism.

  • Joint architectures were compared using a weighted decision matrix and multivoting.
  • Gear ratios were chosen to trade off speed vs. torque for cinematography use-cases.
  • Bearing selection considered both radial loads and packaging around the differential.
  • Belts were used where routing flexibility and low backlash were both acceptable.

Outcomes & what I learned

The final design met the course requirements for workspace, payload, and manufacturability. The differential and worm-gear concepts gave us a clear path to a physical prototype that would be stiff enough for camera work while remaining compact.

Working on this project reinforced how important it is to:

  • Start from realistic motor/gear data instead of idealized torques.
  • Think about assembly and routing (belts, cables, fasteners) from day one.
  • Use selection tools (like multivoting) to keep team decisions objective.
