Tourbillon Watch Mechanism

CAD design of a tourbillon watch movement, including a Swiss lever escapement, balance assembly, and high-ratio gear train inspired by modern haute horlogerie.

Independent project Precision mechanism design AutoCAD Inventor • Gear standards

Overview & goals

This project started as a deep dive into how mechanical watches actually work, beyond just admiring them. I set out to model a functioning tourbillon movement from scratch, using standards from Swiss lever escapements and gear references to make the design plausible—not just visually close.

The design draws aesthetic inspiration from pieces like the Arnold & Son Nebula, but the focus is on the underlying geometry, ratios, and clearances that make an escapement lock, unlock, and impulse correctly.

What I owned

  • Created the escapement and balance assembly geometry in Inventor.
  • Selected tooth counts and modules for the train and cage rotation.
  • Analysed balance inertia and estimated oscillation frequency.
  • Documented key dimensions so parts could theoretically be machined.
Swiss lever escapement Gear train design Balance & hairspring Precision tolerances

Escapement & gear train

I followed horological references and standards to size the escape wheel, pallet fork, and roller jewel. Locking angles, impulse faces, and clearances were chosen so that the pallets would give a clean impulse while still maintaining reliable locking.

The gear train is sized to achieve a realistic beat rate and tourbillon cage rotation. Tooth counts and modules were picked so that:

  • The balance runs at a plausible frequency (e.g., 3–4 Hz range).
  • The cage completes a rotation in an integer multiple of minutes.
  • Individual wheel loads and torques remain sensible for watch scales.
  • Center distances match standard modules from BS/ISO gear data.

Outcomes & takeaways

This project gave me a much deeper appreciation for how unforgiving precision mechanisms are. Small changes in angles or clearances completely change whether the escapement runs, flutters, or just locks up.

From an engineering perspective, it sharpened my understanding of:

  • Translating abstract standards (like lever geometry) into CAD constraints.
  • Designing high-ratio gear trains without losing sight of torque levels.
  • How manufacturing tolerances interact with kinematic intent.
